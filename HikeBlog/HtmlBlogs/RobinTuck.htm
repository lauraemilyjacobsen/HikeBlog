My birthday was on a Wednesday and there was no way I was going to spend it in a cubicle in downtown Seattle with pigeons and crackheads outside. I had something more like this in mind.

Getting an alpine start for a hike on a Monday morning is not easy. I wanted to get a good night's sleep so didn't set an early alarm. Then I had some last-minute work emails to deal with... then I had to pick up the truck. Several hours later, after bouncing through the craters of the moon and fording a creek on the Cle Elum River Road, recalling some unpleasant roads in Nepal and Tibet, we arrived at the trailhead at a leisurely 2:00. A lunch picnic, organizing the gear, and on the trail by 3.

We walked the first, easiest section of the trail to the junction with the "unofficial" trail to Robin and Tuck. Camp was at a pleasant forested spot just past the junction. The next morning, we made quick time uphill to Tuck Lake, a pretty little lake in a granite bowl.

From here, the "trail" is a steep, rocky boot path marked with cairns. It felt a lot longer than its 1.4 miles.

Robin Lakes sit in a basin of smooth, lumpy granite. It's supposed to be a zoo on weekends, but there was only one other party of two middle-aged men when we arrived.

Paul enjoyed bathing his feet in the freezing outlet from Upper to Lower Robin Lake near our campsite.

A family of goats milled around the area between our campsite and the two guys. There were at least 14 individuals. One goat was particularly curious and kept coming back to our campsite despite trying to scare him off with rocks.

As the sun set, the reflections changed from silver to gold.

The lower lake was beautiful on my birthday morning.

Paul peed on a rock on the way up the ridge to watch the sunrise. Goats love pee because of its salt, and hikers are encouraged to urinate on rocks rather than in vegetation since goats will dig up fragile meadow flowers to get at the urine. These goats blocked our exit by licking his urine off the rock right in the place where we wanted to get through.

There were some cute baby goats in the family.

I appreciated the views of Mt. Daniel more on the way down to Tuck Lake. This trail was also a little scarier than the way up. The airy ledges are much more noticable when you're facing downhill. I don't know if trekking poles were more useful or annoying. Some of the descent is best done scooting down crab-style with your hands.

This little lake is called Tuck's Pot. We stopped here for lunch and agreed that with the turquoise water and vivid green trees, it could almost be a jungle pool in Central America.
