Paul was working all weekend, and I made the long drive to Mt. Baker for a weekend of solo hiking. First up was the Chain Lakes loop, which weaves around the jumble of volcanic slopes and alpine lakes around Artist Point. The NOAA forecast kept promising clearer skies shortly, but the blanket of clouds never lifted. At least the gray light threw the slopes of fall color into bright contrast.

Up over Herman Saddle, I noticed a little tarn on a shelf above the trail ahead. I found the boot path leading to the pond, which had an infinity pool effect at its outlet. The background was completely obscured by clouds, giving the reflecting pool a dreamlike quality.

The little pond was so gorgeous that on Sunday, which had bluebird skies, I came back to photograph it in different light. The sunlight brought out the pinkish tones in the rock and made the pond into a deep blue mirror. On the other hand, I like the mystical quality of the lake backed by white clouds.

The nice thing about hiking solo is that I can do unpleasant things in order to get a great photo, without dragging along whiny companions. 4:30 on Sunday morning saw me hauling myself out of the tent at the Douglas Fir campground, boiling up a quick coffee, and driving back up the road. I found a spot on one of the platforms on Picture Lake and watched the mist rise off the lake in the sunrise.

I continued up to the end of the road at Artist Point, got some shots of the sunlight on Mt. Baker, and did the short trail up Table Mountain and back.

 

My other goal before doing the Lake Ann trail was to go back to the little tarn below Herman Saddle that I had photographed in the clouds yesterday. There's a network of criss-crossing trails in the basin below the highway and Table Mountain, and I worked out that the shortest route would start at the Heather Meadows parking lot. This parking lot and the tourist trails just below it are almost obscenely beautiful.

 

I booked it along the Chain Lakes trail, retracing my steps until I found the little tarn, as I said earlier.

By the time I got back to the car, it was past noon, I had already hiked 4-5 miles, without starting the main attraction of the day: Lake Ann. I had a conference call scheduled with my company's India team for 9PM, and with a three-hour drive home, I would have to move fast to do this 8.4 mile hike and get home in time. The elevation profile of this trail is a U shape: it descends steeply in the beginning, follows a flat, wooded valley floor, then climbs up to a pass overlooking the lake, which is perched like an infinity pool on the side of a cliff.

Mt. Shuksan looms over the left side of the lake, with fantastic views of the Upper and Lower Curtis glaciers.

I circumnavigated the lake, completely awed by the panorama. The lower mountain slopes glowed reddish with berry bushes, while the tip of Shuksan gleamed in the clear sky. This is why I put up with the long winters, dreary cityscape, and passive-agressive attitudes in Seattle, for the privilege in being in places that just feel right. This panorama almost shows how beautiful it was.
